REAL WORLD APPLICATIONS

There are many real-world renewable-energy projects being built and used across the globe, demonstrating how clean technologies are transforming the way communities generate and use power. From hydropower and wind farms to solar installations and community-based energy programmes, these initiatives show that sustainable solutions are already practical, effective, and widely adopted. They highlight the worldwide shift toward cleaner electricity, reduced emissions, and greater energy independence, offering clear examples of how renewable energy is supporting both environmental goals and everyday needs.

SOLAR

The UN’s Solar for Health initiative provides reliable, renewable electricity to health facilities in remote and underserved communities by installing solar power systems. By ensuring consistent energy for lighting, vaccine refrigeration, medical equipment, and digital health services, the programme helps clinics operate safely and effectively even where national grids are weak or absent. Solar for Health also reduces operating costs, cuts emissions, and supports local training and maintenance, strengthening both health systems and community resilience.

The SEAI Solar Electricity Grant helps make it easier for households to switch to clean, renewable solar power and enjoy the long-term benefits it offers. By supporting the installation of solar PV panels, the scheme enables homeowners to generate their own electricity, reduce energy bills, and cut their carbon footprint. Solar energy also increases energy independence and provides a reliable power source that works year-round. Overall, the grant encourages more people to embrace a sustainable, climate-friendly energy solution.

The Timahoe North Solar Farm in County Kildare is one of Ireland’s largest solar energy projects, with a capacity of 108 MWp, enough to power around 25,000 homes. Developed by ESB and Bord na Móna, the farm uses thousands of solar panels to convert sunlight into clean, renewable electricity, reducing reliance on fossil fuels and cutting carbon emissions. Beyond generating sustainable energy, the project highlights Ireland’s growing commitment to large-scale solar power and its role in a greener, low-carbon energy future.

WIND

Galway Wind Park is one of the largest onshore wind farms in Ireland, with 58 turbines generating around 174 MW of electricity. It produces enough power to supply tens of thousands of homes, making a significant contribution to the region’s renewable energy supply. The project demonstrates the potential of onshore wind to reduce reliance on fossil fuels and support local communities with clean energy.

Arklow Bank was Ireland's first operational offshore wind farm, located about 10km off the Wicklow coast. With seven turbines producing roughly 25 MW, it showcased the feasibility of harnessing offshore wind energy. The project helped pave the way for larger offshore developments and highlighted the opportunities for generating renewable electricity from Ireland’s coastal winds.

Sorne Hill Wind Farm in County Donegal is an operational onshore wind project with a capacity of around 39 MW. The farm’s turbines generate clean, renewable electricity that helps supply local homes and businesses while reducing reliance on fossil fuels. Sorne Hill demonstrates how smaller-scale wind farms can make a meaningful contribution to Ireland’s renewable energy mix and support the transition to a low-carbon future.

HYDRO

The Shannon Hydroelectric Scheme, completed in the 1920s, was one of Ireland’s earliest and most ambitious national infrastructure projects, harnessing the flow of the River Shannon to generate electricity. Centered at Ardnacrusha, the scheme used a large dam and power station to convert the river’s natural drop into renewable hydropower, dramatically increasing the country’s electricity supply at the time. Its construction marked a major step in Ireland’s modernization, demonstrating the potential of large-scale renewable energy long before it became a global priority.

The Derwent Hydro installation at Cromford Mills forms part of ongoing efforts to restore and demonstrate the site’s historic reliance on water power. Situated within the Derwent Valley Mills UNESCO World Heritage Site, the system uses the flow of the River Derwent to generate renewable electricity much as the original 18th-century waterwheels once powered Richard Arkwright’s pioneering cotton mills. The modern hydro turbine provides a sustainable energy source for the mill complex while showcasing how traditional water-powered industry can be adapted for contemporary environmental goals.

The Poulaphouca Hydroelectric Scheme in County Wicklow harnesses the waters of the River Liffey through the construction of the Poulaphouca Reservoir—Ireland’s largest inland lake—and an associated dam and power station. Developed in the 1930s–40s, the scheme provides renewable electricity by directing controlled water flows through turbines before releasing the water downstream. In addition to power generation, the project also created a vital water supply source for Dublin and helped regulate river levels, making Poulaphouca a key multipurpose asset in Ireland’s hydropower and water-management infrastructure.
